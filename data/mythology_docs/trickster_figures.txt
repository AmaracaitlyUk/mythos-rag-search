Trickster Figures in Mythology

The trickster is one of the most widespread character types across world
mythology: a clever, rule-breaking figure who uses wit rather than strength
to outsmart gods, monsters, or other humans, often causing chaos in the
process. Tricksters frequently occupy a morally ambiguous position, capable
of both helping humanity (bringing fire, teaching skills, or exposing the
foolishness of the powerful) and causing harm through their own greed,
vanity, or mischief.

Examples appear across nearly every mythological tradition: Loki in Norse
mythology, Anansi the spider in West African and Caribbean folklore, Coyote
and Raven in various Native American traditions, and Hermes in Greek
mythology, who as a newborn infant famously stole Apollo's cattle. Scholars
of mythology suggest tricksters serve an important cultural function,
allowing storytellers to question authority, social rules, and the natural
order in a way a purely heroic character could not, while still ultimately
reinforcing the values a society considers important.
