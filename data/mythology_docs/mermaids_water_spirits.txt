Mermaids and Water Spirits

Stories of human-like beings living in or near water appear across
mythologies worldwide, ranging from beautiful, alluring figures to
dangerous, drowning spirits. The mermaid, typically depicted as having a
woman's upper body and a fish's tail, appears in European folklore often as
an ambiguous figure: sometimes benevolent, sometimes luring sailors to their
deaths with enchanting songs, most famously in the Greek myth of the
sirens, who were originally described as bird-like rather than fish-like
beings.

Similar water spirit traditions appear elsewhere: the rusalka of Slavic
folklore, often the restless spirit of a drowned woman, was believed to lure
travelers into rivers, while Japanese folklore describes several water
spirits including the kappa, a mischievous, sometimes dangerous
turtle-like creature said to inhabit rivers and ponds. These water spirit
myths likely reflect a practical concern shared across many riverine and
coastal cultures: bodies of water are genuinely dangerous, and stories
personifying that danger may have served as warnings, particularly to
children, to stay cautious near rivers, lakes, and the sea.
