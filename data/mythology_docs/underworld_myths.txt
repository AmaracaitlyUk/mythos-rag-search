Underworld Myths

Nearly every mythological tradition includes some concept of an underworld,
a realm where the dead go after leaving the world of the living, though the
character of that realm varies enormously between cultures. In Greek
mythology, the underworld ruled by Hades was not simply a place of
punishment but a shadowy, largely neutral resting place for most souls, with
special regions reserved for the especially virtuous or wicked.

Egyptian mythology instead treated the underworld, called Duat, as a
dangerous but navigable journey the soul had to complete, aided by spells
and rituals, culminating in a weighing of the heart against a feather to
determine the soul's fate. Norse mythology split the dead between several
destinations depending on how they died, including Valhalla for warriors
chosen by Odin. A recurring motif across many of these traditions is the
hero's journey into the underworld and back, undertaken by figures such as
Orpheus, Odysseus, and Izanagi, usually to retrieve someone or something
precious at great personal risk.
