Flood Myths Across Cultures

A great flood that destroys most of humanity, survived by a small group who
go on to repopulate the world, appears as a mythological motif across an
unusually wide range of unrelated cultures. The Mesopotamian Epic of
Gilgamesh contains one of the oldest surviving versions, in which a man named
Utnapishtim is warned by a god to build a boat before a devastating flood is
sent to punish humanity.

The biblical story of Noah's Ark follows a similar structure, as does a Hindu
myth in which the god Vishnu, in the form of a fish, warns the sage Manu of
a coming flood and guides his boat to safety. Flood myths also appear in
Mesoamerican, Chinese, and many other traditions worldwide. Scholars
disagree on why this motif recurs so widely: some suggest shared memory of
real ancient floods, others point to the symbolic power of water as an image
of destruction and renewal, and some see it as evidence of cultural
exchange between ancient civilizations along trade routes.
