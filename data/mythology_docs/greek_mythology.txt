Greek Mythology

Greek mythology is the body of stories the ancient Greeks told about their gods,
heroes, and the origin of the world. At its center are the Twelve Olympians,
led by Zeus, who ruled from Mount Olympus after overthrowing his father Cronus
and the earlier generation of Titans. Each god governed a different domain:
Poseidon the sea, Athena wisdom and war strategy, Aphrodite love, and Hades
the underworld.

Beyond the gods, Greek myths are full of mortal heroes whose stories explore
courage, pride, and fate: Heracles and his twelve labors, Perseus slaying
Medusa, Theseus in the labyrinth, and Odysseus's long journey home after the
Trojan War. These myths were not just entertainment; they explained natural
phenomena, justified social customs, and gave the Greeks a shared cultural
framework passed down through oral storytelling long before being written down
by poets like Homer and Hesiod.
