Slavic Mythology

Slavic mythology refers to the pre-Christian religious beliefs of the Slavic
peoples across Eastern Europe, much of it reconstructed from later folklore,
place names, and scattered historical records rather than complete original
texts, since it was never as thoroughly written down as Greek or Norse
mythology. Perun, god of thunder and the sky, is generally considered the
chief deity, often compared to Norse Thor and Greek Zeus, while Veles ruled
over the underworld, cattle, and magic, and was frequently depicted as
Perun's rival.

Slavic folklore is especially rich in household and nature spirits rather
than distant, remote gods: the domovoi was a protective house spirit
believed to live behind the stove, while rusalki were water spirits, often
described as the restless souls of drowned women, who could lure travelers
into rivers and lakes. Baba Yaga, a witch who lives in a hut standing on
chicken legs deep in the forest, remains one of the most recognizable
figures from Slavic folklore, portrayed as dangerous, wise, and unpredictable
depending on the story.
