Hindu Mythology

Hindu mythology is drawn from an enormous body of sacred texts, including the
Vedas, the Puranas, and the great epics Ramayana and Mahabharata, developed
over several thousand years across the Indian subcontinent. Central to Hindu
belief is the Trimurti, three principal deities representing the cycle of
existence: Brahma the creator, Vishnu the preserver, and Shiva the destroyer.

Vishnu is said to descend to earth in different forms, called avatars,
whenever the world falls into serious moral disorder; two of his most famous
avatars, Rama and Krishna, are the heroes of the Ramayana and the
Mahabharata respectively. The Mahabharata in particular contains the Bhagavad
Gita, a philosophical dialogue about duty and righteous action that remains
central to Hindu ethical thought. Hindu mythology is notable for its vast
scale, with countless regional deities, goddesses such as Durga and Lakshmi,
and stories that intertwine cosmic themes with everyday moral guidance.
