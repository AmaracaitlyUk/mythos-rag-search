Dragons in World Mythology

Dragons appear across an enormous range of cultures, but their meaning and
appearance differ sharply between traditions. In most European mythology,
dragons are typically depicted as dangerous, fire-breathing reptilian
monsters that hoard treasure and must be slain by a hero, as in the stories
of Saint George or the dragon Fafnir in Norse legend, which the hero
Sigurd famously kills.

Chinese and broader East Asian traditions instead usually portray dragons as
wise, benevolent, and closely associated with water, rain, and imperial
power rather than destruction; Chinese dragons are traditionally seen as
bringers of good fortune and are strongly linked to rivers and rainfall
important for agriculture. Despite these differences, dragons across many
unrelated cultures share a recurring set of traits: serpentine or reptilian
bodies, immense power, and an association with either treasure, wisdom, or
control over natural forces such as water and weather.
