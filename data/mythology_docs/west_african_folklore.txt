West African Folklore

West African folklore includes a wide range of oral traditions from peoples
such as the Akan, Yoruba, and Ashanti, many of which were later carried
across the Atlantic during the transatlantic slave trade and reshaped into
new folk traditions in the Caribbean and the Americas. One of the
best-known figures is Anansi, a trickster spider from Akan storytelling who
outwits larger and more powerful animals through cleverness rather than
strength, often explaining how various customs or natural features came to
be.

Yoruba mythology describes a large pantheon of orishas, spirits who each
govern a particular domain of nature or human life, such as Shango, god of
thunder and justice, and Oshun, goddess of rivers, love, and beauty; these
orishas remain actively worshipped today in Yoruba religion and in related
traditions such as Santeria and Candomble that developed in the Americas.
West African storytelling traditions historically relied on skilled oral
performers called griots, who preserved history, genealogy, and myth through
memorized song and narration passed down across generations.
