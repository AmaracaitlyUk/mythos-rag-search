Mesopotamian Mythology

Mesopotamian mythology, developed by the Sumerians, Babylonians, and
Assyrians in the region of modern-day Iraq, is among the oldest mythological
traditions to survive in written form, preserved on clay tablets in
cuneiform script. The Enuma Elish, a Babylonian creation epic, describes the
god Marduk defeating the primordial sea goddess Tiamat and forming the
world from her body, establishing his supremacy among the gods.

The Epic of Gilgamesh, centered on the legendary king of Uruk, is one of the
oldest known works of literature and follows Gilgamesh's friendship with the
wild man Enkidu and his desperate, ultimately unsuccessful quest for
immortality after Enkidu's death. Mesopotamian mythology strongly influenced
later Near Eastern traditions, including elements later reflected in
biblical stories, and reveals a worldview deeply concerned with mortality,
the unpredictability of the gods, and humanity's limited control over its
own fate.
