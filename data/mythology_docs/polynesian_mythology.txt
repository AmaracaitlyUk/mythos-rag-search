Polynesian Mythology

Polynesian mythology encompasses the interconnected mythological traditions
of the many island cultures spread across the Pacific, including Hawaii,
Tahiti, Samoa, and New Zealand's Maori people, who share related though
locally distinct pantheons due to their common ancestral origins.
Prominent among the shared deities is Maui, a trickster demigod credited
across many island traditions with feats such as slowing the sun to
lengthen the day, fishing up islands from the ocean floor, and bringing fire
to humanity.

The Hawaiian goddess Pele is closely associated with volcanoes and fire,
reflecting the volcanic geology of the Hawaiian islands themselves, and
remains a culturally significant figure in Hawaii today. Polynesian
mythology places strong emphasis on genealogy and ancestry, with chiefs and
important families often tracing their lineage back to specific gods, and
on the ocean itself, unsurprising given that Polynesian culture depended on
extraordinary feats of long-distance ocean navigation between widely
scattered islands.
