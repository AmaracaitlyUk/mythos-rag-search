Celtic Mythology

Celtic mythology refers to the pre-Christian mythological traditions of the
Celtic peoples across Ireland, Wales, Scotland, and parts of continental
Europe, much of it preserved through medieval Irish and Welsh manuscripts
after being passed down orally for centuries. Irish mythology in particular
describes several waves of mythical settlers, most notably the Tuatha De
Danann, a supernatural race of gods and heroes said to have ruled Ireland
before retreating into the hollow hills and becoming the fairy folk of later
folklore.

Prominent figures include Lugh, a many-skilled warrior god, and the Morrigan,
a goddess associated with war, fate, and prophecy who could appear as a crow
on the battlefield. Welsh mythology, recorded in a collection known as the
Mabinogion, tells its own distinct cycle of stories involving kings,
enchantments, and shape-shifting. Celtic myths place heavy emphasis on the
Otherworld, a supernatural realm existing alongside the human world and
sometimes reachable through mist, water, or ancient burial mounds.
